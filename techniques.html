Master the Strokes

Swimming efficiency comes from minimizing drag and maximizing propulsion. Explore the mechanics of the four competitive strokes.

Freestyle (Front Crawl)

The Mechanics

  • Body Position: Keep your head down, eyes looking at the bottom of the pool. A high head drops your hips, creating drag.
  • The Catch: Enter the water fingertips first, reaching forward. hinge at the elbow to "catch" the water early (Early Vertical Forearm).
  • The Pull: Accelerate your hand backward past your hip. Do not slip the water; hold onto it like a ladder rung.
  • The Kick: A steady 6-beat flutter kick drives rotation. Initiate the kick from the hips, not the knees.

Common Mistakes

Crossing Over: Hand entering the water crosses the center line of the body. This causes the hips to snake side-to-side.
Dropping the Elbow: Pulling with the elbow leading the hand strips all power from the stroke. Keep high elbows!

Training Drill: Zipper Drill

During recovery, drag your thumb up the side of your torso (like zipping up a jacket) until it reaches your armpit, then extend forward. This enforces a high elbow and proper body rotation.

Butterfly

Often considered the most difficult stroke, butterfly relies entirely on rhythm and undulation. It is a wave motion that starts at the chest and flows through the toes.

Key Components

The Undulation
Press your chest down as your hips rise, then snap your hips down as your chest rises. This wave drives propulsion.
The Double Kick
Two kicks per cycle: One big kick when hands enter the water, one smaller kick when hands exit to propel recovery.
Low Recovery
Arms should sweep low over the water surface using the momentum of the hips, not just shoulder strength.

Pro Tips

  • Breathe late in the pull, but get your head down BEFORE your arms enter the water.
  • Keep your chin on the water surface when breathing—don't look at the ceiling.
  • Think "Press T" – Press your chest down to pop your hips up.

Backstroke

Technique Breakdown

Rotation is King: You shouldn't swim flat on your back. Rotate 45 degrees to each side. This allows the shoulder to come out of the water cleanly and the other arm to pull deeper.

Pinky In, Thumb Out: Enter the water pinky first to set up the catch. Rotate the hand during the pull so you exit thumb first.

Head Position: Keep your head perfectly still. Imagine balancing a glass of chocolate milk on your forehead. If you look at your toes, your hips will sink.

The "Tea Cup" Drill

Goal: Stabilize the head position.

Swim backstroke with a half-filled cup of water (or a small forehead bottle) balanced on your forehead. The goal is to rotate your hips and shoulders without spilling the water or letting the object fall comfortably.

Breaststroke

Timing is Everything

Order matters: Pull, Breathe, Kick, Glide. Unlike other strokes, there is a distinct dead spot in breaststroke—the glide—which is actually where you travel the fastest.

The Kick

The "Whip Kick". Bring heels towards your butt, turn feet OUT (dorsiflexion), and whip them around in a circle until they snap together. The power comes from the snap/squeeze at the end.

Underwaters

Breaststroke creates the most drag, so the underwater pullout is critical.

Steps for a perfect Pullout:

  1. Push off wall, streamline.
  2. One dolphin kick.
  3. Full arm pull down to thighs (keyhole pull).
  4. Recover hands close to body, sneak them up.
  5. Kick and break surface.
