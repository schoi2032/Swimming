Ancient Beginnings

Swimming is as old as humanity. The earliest record of swimming dates back to Stone Age paintings from around 10,000 years ago in the Cave of Swimmers near Wadi Sora in southwestern Egypt.

Written references date from 2000 B.C., including in the Epic of Gilgamesh, the Iliad, and the Bible. In ancient Greece and Rome, swimming was a part of martial training and element of education. Plato once said that a man who doesn't know how to swim is uneducated.

The Competitive Era

[1896 Olympics Placeholder]

Swimming emerged as a competitive sport in the early 19th century in England. The National Swimming Society, formed in London in 1837, held regular competitions.

The First Olympics (1896): Swimming was part of the first modern Olympic games in Athens. Four events were held: 100m, 500m, and 1200m freestyle, plus a sailors' event. All were held in the open ocean (Bay of Zea).

The Crawl: The stroke we know as "Freestyle" wasn't popularized until the early 1900s. It was adapted from the "Australian Crawl," which itself was inspired by the swimming style of South Pacific islanders.

Modern Evolution

The 20th century saw massive leaps in technique and technology.

  • 1956: Butterfly Born

    The butterfly stroke evolved from breaststroke (recovering arms out of water) and was finally recognized as a separate stroke for the Melbourne Olympics.

  • 1976: Goggles

    Goggles were first allowed in Olympic competition in 1976, revolutionizing training volume since chlorine no longer destroyed eyes.

  • 2008: Super Suits

    The poly-urethane "tech suit" era smashed nearly every world record before being banned in 2010 to preserve the integrity of the sport.
